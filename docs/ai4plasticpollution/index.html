In 1907, Belgian chemist, Leo Baekeland, patented the first fully synthetic plastic. He named it Bakelite. Then, by accident, polyethylene was discovered again in the 1930s (after the accidental invention in 1898). Its first use was insulating radar cabling during the 2nd world war. But soon, it was used in consumer products like Tupperware, shopping bags, etc. Then came polyethylene terephthalate (PET) in 1973. It along with polyethylene bags and other plastics increased the usage of single-use plastics by many folds. And our plastic problems got exponentially worse.

Today, plastic pollution is one of the most critical environmental hazards. Sadly, the properties of plastic which make it so useful, also make it a curse to our environment. A plastic bottle can last for more than 4 centuries in oceans. Then too, it only breaks down to microscopic particles and never really goes away. Writing just about the intensity of plastic pollution can be a blog in itself. Nowadays, plastics can be found in the Antarctic ice and our bodies too!!

Tracking Plastic Waste

At The Ocean Cleanup project, neural networks are helping them to map and monitor the density of plastic waste in the ocean. Their objective is to determine optimal locations for cleanup operations. They use time-lapsed GPS-tagged images to train a convolutional neural network to perform object detection over more than 400 plastic items. If interested, you can also read the detailed write-up.

Global Plastic Watch is a platform that maps plastic pollution using satellite images and AI. They have identified 2,802 plastic waste sites in 25 countries. You can visit their website to explore the data yourself. This data can help prioritize higher-risk waste sites. Data contains really useful attributes like site area, distance to the water body, area population, etc.

Separating Plastics

Hera SpA partnered with IBM to reduce landfill waste by redirecting useful reusable materials to appropriate channels. They aim to use computer vision algorithms to sort waste into various categories. The IBM website has a detailed blog on this if you are interested.

AI tech in machines identifies different kinds of plastic automatically and stores them accordingly. This fast-tracks plastic reuse.

Plastic Decomposition

PET accounts for 12% of global solid waste. It can be effectively reused by breaking this polymer into smaller fragments. This is called depolymerization. At The University of Texas at Austin, researchers created an enzyme to do just that. They used a machine learning algorithm to design robust and active enzymes. This research was published in Nature. ColdFusion created an amazing video explaining this research.

Similar efforts are done by scientists at the Centre for Enzyme Innovation at the University of Portsmouth. They are using Deepmind‚Äôs alphafold to analyze various protein structures to come up with effective enzymes to decompose plastics.

Final Thoughts

AI is just a tool. It can help us but is not the solution. A lot of steps are been taken to deal with this problem. Campaigns like clean seas are instrumental in fighting against plastic pollution. We can also contribute our few cents towards this cause. Some ways you can do that are:

  • Carry reusable bags whenever you go shopping.
  • Refill empty plastic bottles whenever possible.
  • Try to reduce the usage of single-use plastics.
  • Choose plastic-free personal care products, like a bamboo comb.

Companies are using machine learning to do efficient and sustainable packaging. ML techniques can detect defects in packaging and suggest optimal packaging. The European Union estimated that over 80% of all product-related environmental impacts are determined during the design phase of a product. By using ML, we can optimize package design to use less plastic.
Keeping eye on plastic waste and monitoring it at scale is a daunting task. This is a very good candidate for an ML use case. Initiatives like ‚ÄùThe Ocean Cleanup project‚Ä≥ are using deep learning to identify and quantify plastic litter in oceans and rivers. Locating large plastic dumps on Earth can help us in planning plastic management.

Deep learning models identify plastic and robotic arms are used to separate them from the rest of the pile. Robots can work longer and faster without tiring, increasing efficiency many folds.
What if plastics can be decomposed? What if they take just a few days to decompose rather than years?
